4.1 Deformation Of Solids

4.1.1 Hookes Law

Hooke's Law - The restoring force is directly proportional to the extension, provided the limit of proportionality is not exceeded.

4.1.2 Elastic Deformation + Plastic Deformation

Elastic Deformation - when the load is removed the material will return to its original length/shape. The material may or may not follow Hooke's law.
Plastic Deformation - The material is extendable but does not follow Hooke's law, any extension is permanent.

4.1.3 Limit of Proportionality Vs Elastic limit

The Limit Of Proportionality - The point to which Hooke's law applies.
The Elastic Limit - The point after which the material will be permanently extended when unloading.

4.1.4 Stress, Strain + The Young Modulus

Stress - the amount of force applied to a solid over a given area.
Strain - the ratio of extension to original length.

4.2 Thermal Physics

4.2.1 Gas Laws

Boyle's Law - for a fixed mass of gas at constant temperature, the volume is inversely proportional to the applied pressure.
Charles Law - for a fixed mass of gas at constant pressure, the volume is directly proportional to the Kelvin temperature.
Gay-Lussac Law - for a fixed mass of gas at constant volume, the pressure is directly proportional to the Kelvin temperature.

4.2.4 Internal Energy

Internal Energy - For a real gas The sum of the potential and kinetic energy for its molecules.
Internal Energy - For an ideal gas The sum of the kinetic energy for its molecules.

4.2.7 Absolute Zero

Absolute Zero - The temperature at which all molecular motion stops approx -273.16°C (for calculations -273°C is sufficient).

4.2.8 Specific Heat Capacity

Specific Heat Capacity - The quantity of heat energy needed to rise the temperature of 1kg of the material by 1k.

4.3 Uniform Circular Motion

4.3.1 Angular Velocity

Angular Velocity - The angle turned through over the time taken.

4.4 Simple Harmonic Motion

Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM) - If an object's acceleration is directly proportional to its displacement from its rest position and is always directed towards that position then it is undergoing simple harmonic motion.

4.4.6 Free Vibration

Free vibration - Anything that is vibrating without an external driving force. It will oscillate at its natural frequency. Natural Frequency - This is the frequency that an oscillator will vibrate at when allowed to freely oscillate. Denoted as $$f_o$$. Forced Oscillation - A vibration that is being made to happen by an external driving force. Driving frequency - The frequency of the application of the force in a forced oscillation. Resonance - The phenomenon that occurs when the driving frequency is equal to the natural frequency. This leads to the maximum of transfer of energy into the system.

Dampening

Light damping - this is when the oscillation comes to rest but performs a negative displacement in the process. Critical damping - The oscillation comes to rest without oscillating in the fastest possible this is given by $$\frac{T}{4}$$. Heavy / Over damping - the oscillation comes to rest without oscillating in a time greater than $$\frac{T}{4}$$.
